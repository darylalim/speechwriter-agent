# Delivery and Cadence: Writing for the Ear

## Overview
Spoken words reach the audience once, in real time, with no chance to re-read. This skill converts written-style prose into speakable prose — lines a person can deliver on one breath and a listener can follow on first hearing. Apply it to every draft bound for a podium, camera, or microphone.

## When to Use
- The output will be spoken aloud: speech, toast, keynote, eulogy, pitch, announcement, video script.
- A draft reads fine on the page but feels stiff, breathless, or clumsy when voiced.
- The speaker needs a marked-up script with pacing cues.
- You are placing or sharpening a landing line (the memorable takeaway).

## Instructions

**Write spoken English, not written English.**
- Always use contractions: "we will not" → "we won't."
- One idea per sentence. Break compound sentences at the conjunction.
- Cut subordinate-clause pile-ups.
- *Before:* "Although the initiative, which launched in March, faced significant headwinds, we nonetheless achieved a 40% increase in enrollment." *After:* "We launched in March. The headwinds were real. And still — enrollment jumped forty percent."

**Vary sentence length.** String medium sentences, then drop a short one to land the point. Even length lulls; a sudden three-word sentence wakes the room. "It's not a policy. It's not a slogan. It's a promise."

**Front-load the key word.** Put the punch at the start or end, never buried mid-sentence. "Courage is what this moment demands" beats "This moment demands, among other things, courage."

**Signpost structure aloud.** The ear can't see paragraph breaks, so voice them: "Here's the thing…", "So what does that mean for us?", "Three reasons.", "But then something changed."

**Use repetition and the pause.** Repeat a stem to build: "We rebuilt the line. We rebuilt the trust. We rebuilt the town." Mark deliberate silence before or after a key phrase — the pause is punctuation for the voice.

**Read every line aloud — this is the primary edit pass.** If you stumble, run out of breath, or trip on consonants, rewrite. Punctuation is pacing: a period is a full stop, a comma a short breath, a dash a beat, an ellipsis a held silence.

**Kill tongue-twisters and unspeakable numbers.**
- Avoid clustered sibilants and plosives: "statistics suggest six succinct steps" is a trap.
- Voice figures as a speaker would: "$1,347,892" → "nearly one and a half million dollars"; "72.4%" → "roughly three in four."

**Place landing lines with intent.** End each section and the whole speech on a short, concrete, front-loaded line. Set it up, pause, deliver it clean — no trailing clause to smother it.

**Format the script for the speaker.**
- Short lines, one thought per line, generous whitespace.
- Bracketed cues in a consistent style: `[pause]`, `[slow]`, `[louder]`, `[beat]`, `[look up]`.
```
We had one shot.  [pause]
One.  [slow]
And we took it — together.  [beat, look up]
```

## Pitfalls
- Comma-spliced sentences that leave the speaker gasping.
- Formal register no one says aloud: "utilize," "aforementioned," "in order to."
- Numbers read digit-by-digit or with false precision.
- Rhetorical questions with no pause after them.
- Landing lines buried mid-paragraph or weakened by a trailing "…which is why we should all care."
- Trusting how it looks on screen instead of reading it aloud.
- Over-cueing: brackets on every line drown the ones that matter.
