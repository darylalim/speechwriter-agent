# Rhetorical Devices for Memorable Speech

## Overview
A working toolkit of rhetorical figures that make spoken lines land, stick, and get quoted. Use it while drafting to add rhythm and while revising to sharpen a flat passage. Reach for one device per key moment — never stack them.

## When to Use
- The draft is clear but forgettable and needs rhythm or a quotable line.
- You are writing an opening hook, a peroration (close), or a deliberate applause beat.
- A sentence carries the speech's core contrast, promise, or call to action.
- The speaker asked for something "punchier," "more presidential," or "more memorable."

## Instructions
Apply devices only to load-bearing sentences. Read every candidate aloud; if it does not sound good spoken, cut it. Each entry gives the figure, an example, and its cue.

**Tricolon (rule of three)** — Three parallel items; the third lands hardest.
- *"We were doubted, we were dismissed, and now we are unstoppable."*
- Use when: closing a paragraph or building to a climax.

**Anaphora** — Repeat the same words at the start of successive clauses.
- *"Not when the well runs dry. Not when the lights go out. Not while I am standing here."*
- Use when: building emotional momentum across a list.

**Epistrophe** — Repeat the same words at the end of successive clauses.
- *"The work is ours. The risk is ours. The future is ours."*
- Use when: hammering one idea home; pause before the last line.

**Antithesis** — Balance two opposed ideas in parallel structure.
- *"We build bridges where others build walls."*
- Use when: framing a choice or contrasting your side with the alternative.

**Chiasmus** — Reverse the grammatical order of two paired phrases (ABBA).
- *"Quitters never plan, and planners never quit."*
- Use when: you want a line that feels like earned wisdom.

**Antimetabole** — Chiasmus that repeats the exact words in reverse.
- *"We do not fear the future; we make the future we do not fear."*
- Use when: crafting the single most quotable line.

**Asyndeton** — Omit conjunctions for speed and urgency.
- *"They came, they organized, they won."*
- Use when: conveying momentum or decisiveness.

**Polysyndeton** — Add conjunctions for weight and abundance.
- *"And the farmers and the nurses and the teachers and the coders all showed up."*
- Use when: emphasizing scale, inclusion, or relentlessness.

**Parallelism** — Match grammatical structure across phrases.
- *"To listen honestly, to argue fairly, to decide together."*
- Use when: any list; it is the base most other devices build on.

**Metaphor / simile** — Map the abstract onto the concrete (simile uses *like*/*as*).
- *"This budget is a lifeboat, not a luxury cruise."*
- Use when: making a statistic tangible. One controlling image per speech.

**Allusion** — A brief, recognizable reference to shared culture or history.
- *"This is our own moon-shot moment."*
- Use when: borrowing gravitas — only if the audience shares the reference.

**Rhetorical question** — A question posed for effect, not an answer.
- *"How much longer do we wait?"*
- Use when: pivoting to a call to action; answer it yourself immediately.

**Planted applause line** — A short, self-contained, values-affirming line built for a pause.
- *"This town was never for sale — and it never will be."*
- Use when: you need an applause beat. Keep it under a dozen words, end on a stressed syllable, and pause after.

## Pitfalls
- **Overuse:** Stacking devices in every sentence exhausts the ear. Space them; let plain sentences breathe between flourishes.
- **Mixed metaphors:** Never let one image collide with another (*"a lifeboat steering the ship toward a bright dawn"*). Pick one and extend it cleanly.
- **Forced sound patterns:** If alliteration or rhyme warps the meaning or sounds gimmicky, cut it — clarity beats cleverness.
- **Silent applause lines:** A planted line works only if delivery stops after it; mark the beat in the script.
- **Obscure allusions:** A reference the audience misses reads as elitist or confusing. When in doubt, cut it.
